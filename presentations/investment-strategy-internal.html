Enterprise Integration

Read-only architecture reduces operational risk; contracts still matter.

Technical boundary

Connector credentials are tenant-scoped. Egress controls permit reads, not operational write-back. Signals flow through ingestion, Kafka, the graph engine, PostgreSQL, API, and portal.

Commercial and legal boundary

Each deployment still requires customer authorization, source-system terms review, data-processing agreements, access controls, retention rules, and security review. We do not characterize integration as categorically barrier-free.

Construction

Schedule and milestone status from systems such as Procore and BuildPro.

Mortgage & title

Status signals, not underwriting, settlement, or lending decisions.

CRM & financial

Contract, close-date, and operational status needed for cross-system visibility.

Positioning: an authorized analytics layer above existing systems — never a replacement and never an autonomous actor inside builder tools.

Prediction Mechanics

Useful today; production learning must prove what generalizes.

Cross-system progress

Detect when construction, mortgage, and title are moving at incompatible rates. A home can look healthy inside one system and still be at risk across the full path to close.

Signal velocity and staleness

Measure recent activity and source freshness per deal. Silence is a feature to test, not a claim that it is universally the strongest predictor.

Neighborhood and builder baselines

Compare a home with similar communities, products, seasons, and lifecycle stages using the builder's own history where available.

Explainability and intervention

Rank contributing factors and pair them with a human-owned next action. Production data must validate calibration, precision, drift, and whether additional model mechanisms are needed.

Hiring implication: fund ML/platform engineering to review feature validity and retrain on real builder outcomes. Current simulated-data performance is product evidence, not proof of production ROI.

Internal Appendix Decision

Blockchain is an option, not a product dependency.

Decision

Omit it from the core investor thesis and near-term roadmap. Confidence scoring, escalation, and the audit log work without a distributed ledger. Revisit only if several customers or title/recording authorities request shared verification.

Market pull is the trigger. There is no arbitrary two- or three-year restart date.

Three different legal questions

Private audit trail: ordinary enterprise data-provenance use; not inherently a security.

Blockchain-recorded deed: subject to state property, notarization, filing-format, and local recording procedures.

Fractional investment token: securities law may apply when the economic arrangement is an investment contract; changing the record format does not avoid that analysis.

Sources: federal E-SIGN statute; Arizona blockchain statute; SEC digital-asset investment-contract framework. State and local rules remain transaction-specific; obtain counsel before deployment.

Revenue & Sales Strategy

Land a division, prove ROI, then expand through the builder.

1 · Paid Pilot

One division

Scope 500–2,000 active homes, 2–3 systems, a 90-day connector target, and agreed baseline/outcome definitions. Charge against monitored homes.

2 · Proof

Case study

Validate data freshness, prediction calibration, workflow adoption, escalations acted on, and exposure avoided. Do not promise savings before measurement.

3 · Expansion

Enterprise rollout

Add divisions and system categories under an annual commitment priced at the $100/home equivalent, with volume and implementation terms negotiated.

Regional example

2,400 annual homes × $100 = $240K ACV, compared with a planning exposure scenario of $907K/year. That is a hypothesis to validate, not a guaranteed return.

National ceiling example

D.R. Horton's 87,168 homes × $100 = $8.7M ACV at full adoption. This replaces the unsupported prior example.

GTM spending gate: founder-led selling first; add dedicated sales capacity only after two paid pilots and one credible case study.

What drives cost

Production high availability creates a fixed Kafka/RDS/network baseline. At 250K signals/day, seven-day Kafka storage at RF3 is only ~10.5GB; PostgreSQL can grow roughly 548–913GB/year with indexes and overhead.

Required before national scale

RF3 Kafka with 12–24 partitions; multiple graph consumers; PgBouncer and explicit pools; RDS Multi-AZ; time-partitioned signals; closed-home graph archival; S3/Parquet history and derived ML features.

Planning assumptions: AWS us-east-1 on-demand, ARM Fargate, 2KB average signal, 2–5 signals/active-home/day, seven-day Kafka retention. Strategic outcomes

Independent vertical platform: continue while retention, expansion, margins, and prediction value strengthen.

Strategic acquisition: evaluate only when an acquirer values the integration network, workflow position, and data advantage more highly than the standalone plan.

No artificial floor: do not present an acqui-hire estimate as guaranteed downside protection.

Primary risks: security review delays, source-system access, incomplete data, weak model lift, long enterprise sales cycles, and willingness to pay. Mitigate with paid pilots, scoped integrations, telemetry, and milestone-gated spending — not unsupported probability estimates.
